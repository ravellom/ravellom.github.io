Explainable AI in Education: Structural Limits, Human Oversight, and the Role of Pedagogical Design
Abstract
The rapid incorporation of artificial intelligence (AI) tools into educational settings has intensified debates about teacher responsibility, transparency, and human oversight. While explainable AI (XAI) is increasingly recognized as a necessary condition for responsible use, this paper argues that explainability alone may not be sufficient in contexts where complex systems evolve and operate continuously. Even when individual recommendations are accompanied by coherent explanations, the cumulative effects of algorithmic decisions can shape learning trajectories in ways that are difficult to fully monitor. Building on recent discussions in AI governance and educational design, the paper examines the structural limits of monitoring advanced AI systems in classrooms and proposes that pedagogical architecture must complement technical transparency. In particular, Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is discussed not as an alternative to XAI, but as a design framework that reinforces explainability by promoting diversified instructional pathways and reducing excessive dependency on a single algorithmic logic. To illustrate these principles, the paper presents a supervised exercise generation tool that integrates pedagogical invariants, controlled variability, progressive explainability, and explicit human review requirements. The example demonstrates how explainability can be embedded not only in algorithmic outputs but in the architecture of instructional design itself. The paper concludes that educational contexts may benefit from prioritizing supervised, modular, and explainable AI systems that preserve professional judgment and pedagogical pluralism, rather than increasingly autonomous architectures that risk narrowing the space for human decision-making.
1. Introduction
In recent years, many tools that claim to "use AI" have arrived in schools, platforms that recommend activities, correct exercises, detect dropout risks or generate automatic reports. In all cases there is one thing that does not change: although the tool is very sophisticated, the responsibility for what happens in the classroom continues to lie with the teacher and the school (European Commission, 2025).
That is why there is an increasing interest on explainable artificial intelligence (XAI). In a simple way, an AI is explainable when it can answer questions such as: "why have you proposed this activity to this student and not another?", "what did you base this grade on?" or "what data are you using to make this recommendation?" (Bellas, 2025). These explanations are not a technical whim, they allow teachers to accept, nuance or correct the decisions of the tool, and justify their own decisions to students, families and decision-makers (Dwivedi et al., 2023). 
The recent European report on explainable AI in education (European Commission, 2025) insists on precisely this: explainability is a condition for human oversight and shared responsibility. That is, so that the different actors (administration, schools, management teams and teachers) can understand what the system does, intervene when necessary and ensure that AI does not replace professional judgement but supports it.​
However, at the same time that these ideas are consolidated, the field of AI security warns of a less visible problem: there are systems so complex that it is very difficult, and even theoretically impossible, to always anticipate how they will behave in all situations (Yampolskiy, 2025). Some models change with updates, show new capabilities when used at scale, or react unexpectedly to small changes in context (Azadi, 2025). Even if they give us explanations of their decisions, that does not mean that we can foresee everything they will do.
In education, where we do not work with anonymous clicks but with learning trajectories and identities in formation, this uncertainty has a special weight. It is not only a question of understanding why today the system has proposed a specific worksheet, but also of how small decisions, repeated over time, can influence students' confidence, their self-perception of competence and the opportunities offered to them.​
This raises a broader issue of monitorability, the extent to which complex AI systems can be meaningfully observed and anticipated in their evolving behavior. Given these structural limits of monitorability, we argue that the response cannot rely exclusively on improving the internal explainability of AI systems. It must also involve the pedagogical design of classroom practices and tool integration strategies. By “design,” we do not refer to curriculum reform or administrative restructuring, but to the deliberate configuration of learning activities, assessment strategies, and tool integration practices that prevent excessive dependency on a single algorithmic logic. In this sense, classroom and institutional design become complementary safeguards that reinforce human oversight and pedagogical pluralism in AI-mediated environments.
Such design-oriented safeguards can draw on established pedagogical frameworks that promote variability, pluralism, and multiple pathways for engagement and assessment. For instance, approaches such as Universal Design for Learning (UDL) offer structured principles for diversifying instructional design in ways that can complement and reinforce explainability and human oversight in AI-mediated contexts. However, in this paper, UDL is not treated as an alternative to XAI, but as a pedagogical architecture that can make explainable AI pedagogically actionable and aligned with professional judgment in everyday practice.
Throughout the text, we first clarify what it means for AI to be explainable in educational contexts and why this is central to teacher responsibility. We then examine structural limits to monitoring and controlling certain advanced AI systems. Finally, we explore how pedagogical design frameworks, such as UDL, can reinforce the practical effectiveness of XAI by promoting diversified and resilient classroom practices, ensuring that AI remains a support rather than the sole instructional pathway.
2. What does it mean for an AI to be explainable in education?
When we talk about AI in education, we usually think of tools that recommend activities, correct exercises, detect difficulties, or generate automatic reports. Behind each of these actions are decisions such as which task to propose, what grade to assign, and who is marked as "at risk." Explainability has to do with something very simple: that the system can provide understandable reasons for making those decisions, and that those reasons make sense to the teacher (Gomez et al., 2026). One of the emerging approaches to strengthening trust in these systems is Explainable Artificial Intelligence, understood as a set of methods that allow human users to understand, adequately trust, and effectively manage AI systems (Miller, 2019). 
Early work in Explainable AI focused primarily on algorithms and on interpretability as a property of the model itself. From a design perspective, models with high intrinsic interpretability, such as decision trees, rule-based systems, or general additive models, are desirable because their reasoning can be directly inspected and communicated. Conversely, more complex models, such as ensemble methods, support vector machines, or deep neural networks, are not easily interpretable and therefore require post-hoc techniques to approximate the explanations of their predictions (Barredo Arrieta et al., 2020). 
In educational settings, explainability cannot be reduced solely to the transparency of the model; it must be aligned with the pedagogical context and with the interpretive capacities and responsibilities of educators. Only then can explanations genuinely support professional judgment instead of simply embellishing automated decisions with technical details.
2.1. Two visual examples to understand explainability
One educational example of XAI in education is shown in Figure 2, which contains an snapshot of an e-learning platform for Secondary School that assigns exercises adapted to the level of each student (Ooge, 2023). This combination of textual and visual explanation helps the teacher to understand why the system proposes certain tasks and if this proposal fits with what he or she observes in class.

These cases show two important ideas: that explanations can take different formats (text, graphics, visual examples) and that a good explanation is not just "what result the AI has given", but "what it has looked at" to arrive at that result. From here, we can further specify what we mean by explainable AI in education and why this is important for teacher accountability.
2.2. Four key ideas of explainability
To organize this topic, four key characteristics are usually analyzed (Ali et al., 2023; Bellas, 2025):
Transparency: refers to the human capacity to understand the processes of an AI system. Traditional AI models often function as "black boxes," making it difficult to discern how decisions are made. In education, it is knowing what the tool does, what it is for, and what data it works with. For example, if a system uses only class notes or also data on behavior, attendance, etc.
Interpretability: That someone with technical knowledge can review the model and its internal functioning. This is less visible to the teacher, but important for external experts to audit the tool.
Explainability: that the system can give understandable reasons for specific decisions ("why this activity, why this note"). It is what most directly affects the teacher.
Comprehensibility: that these explanations are given in a language and format that can really be understood by the specific user: in this case, the teacher, the management team, the student or their family.
For a classroom teacher, the last two are the most relevant: it is not much use for the tool to be "explainable" if the explanations come in the form of formulas or graphs that no one in the school can interpret.
Why is it key to teacher responsibility?
In practice, explainability is directly linked to professional responsibility. Even if a company has developed the tool, the one who decides to use it in the classroom and trust (or not) its recommendations is the teacher and the school (Roddeck et al., 2025). This decision has consequences, it can influence what tasks students perform, how they are evaluated and what messages they receive about their own performance.
If teachers cannot ask "why?" to the tool, their margin of supervision is reduced. On the other hand, when AI offers clear explanations:
the teacher can check if the recommendation fits with his pedagogical criteria or if it should be adjusted;
can detect errors (e.g., a clearly unfair note) and correct them with arguments;
can explain to students and families what role the tool has played in a particular decision.
From this perspective, explainability is not an optional addition, but the minimum condition for AI not to replace teacher judgment, but to put itself at its service (Khosravi et al., 2022).
In the following sections we will go a step further and ask an uncomfortable but important question, even when a tool offers good explanations, can we be sure that we will always see in time where their decisions are leading our students as a whole?
3. When Explainability Is Not Enough: Structural Limits to Monitoring AI in Education
So far, we have emphasized the importance of explainability as a condition for teacher responsibility and human oversight. However, even when an AI system provides coherent and understandable explanations for individual decisions, this does not automatically guarantee that its broader influence over time can be fully anticipated or monitored.
Many contemporary AI-based educational tools are dynamic systems that evolve through updates, retraining processes, or adjustments in internal weighting mechanisms. These modifications do not necessarily imply that the system becomes less accurate or less reliable. On the contrary, they may improve performance according to specific metrics. Yet from the perspective of classroom practice, even subtle changes in how recommendations are calculated can alter the educational experience in ways that are not immediately visible. A teacher may receive locally convincing explanations for each recommendation, while the cumulative pattern of suggestions gradually shifts across weeks or months.
This distinction between local explainability and global trajectory is particularly significant in education. Teaching does not consist of isolated decisions but of sequences of pedagogical choices that shape students’ learning pathways over time. A recommendation for reinforcement today can influence tomorrow’s content exposure, groupings, assessment opportunities, and expectations regarding a student’s ability. Each individual decision may be explainable and pedagogically defensible, yet the aggregated effect of many such decisions may contribute to unintended trajectories. Students might be repeatedly directed toward less demanding activities, systematically categorized as “at risk,” or subtly steered toward narrower forms of participation without any single decision appearing problematic in isolation.
Furthermore, the increasing complexity of advanced AI systems introduces additional epistemic limits. As models grow in scale and are deployed in diverse contexts, they may exhibit behaviors that were not fully anticipated during their design or validation phases. These behaviors do not necessarily reflect malfunction, but rather the inherent difficulty of exhaustively predicting system responses across evolving real-world conditions. Explanations may accurately describe why a particular output was generated under specific parameters, yet they do not always provide insight into how the system’s behavior may adapt when those parameters change or when contextual variables differ substantially from the training data.
The educational implications of this structural uncertainty are substantial. When classroom activity becomes highly dependent on algorithmic recommendations, determining which tasks are assigned, how progress is interpreted, or which students are flagged for intervention, the AI system effectively becomes a central organizing force in the learning environment. In such cases, even if each decision remains explainable, the overall pedagogical direction may be increasingly shaped by an evolving algorithmic logic that is only partially visible to educators.
For teachers, the challenge is therefore not limited to asking why a specific recommendation was made. It also involves reflecting on how patterns of recommendations accumulate, how shifts in system behavior may influence classroom dynamics, and how much room remains for professional judgment to redirect or counterbalance algorithmic guidance. These questions do not imply that AI systems inevitably deteriorate over time. Rather, they highlight that explainability at the level of individual decisions does not fully resolve the broader issue of monitoring and steering complex systems within long-term educational processes.
Recognizing these limits does not undermine the value of explainable AI. Instead, it clarifies that explainability, while necessary, may not be sufficient to guarantee pedagogical resilience in contexts where algorithmic systems operate continuously and evolve over time. This recognition opens the door to complementary strategies that strengthen classroom design and reduce excessive dependency on a single algorithmic pathway.
4. Universal Design for Learning as a Pedagogical Safeguard in AI-Mediated Education
The limits discussed in the previous section do not invalidate the importance of explainable AI. Rather, they highlight that explainability alone may not be sufficient to ensure pedagogical resilience in environments where algorithmic systems operate continuously and evolve over time. If AI systems can influence learning trajectories through repeated, cumulative decisions, then classroom practice cannot rely exclusively on the transparency of individual outputs. It must also incorporate structural safeguards within pedagogical design.
Universal Design for Learning (CAST, 2026) offers a relevant framework in this regard. Originally developed to address learner variability (Roski et al., 2021), UDL proposes that educational environments should provide multiple means of representation, action and expression, and engagement (Wilson et al., 2025). Its core assumption is that variability is not an exception to be corrected but a fundamental characteristic of learning contexts. When instructional design anticipates variability, it reduces the risk that any single pathway becomes determinative of student success.
In the context of AI-mediated education, this principle acquires a new dimension. If a classroom relies predominantly on a single AI system to assign tasks, evaluate performance, or structure progression, then the algorithm effectively becomes the primary organizer of learning. Even when the system is explainable, such centrality increases dependency on a particular algorithmic logic. By contrast, a classroom design aligned with UDL principles intentionally incorporates diversified instructional routes. Algorithmically generated activities can coexist with teacher-designed tasks, collaborative projects, oral presentations, or alternative assessment formats addressing the same objectives. The presence of multiple pedagogical pathways reduces the risk that an evolving AI system becomes the sole determinant of educational trajectories.
Under these conditions, explainability gains practical depth. Explanations provided by the AI system are interpreted within a broader instructional ecology rather than in isolation. Teachers can accept, adapt, or override recommendations while relying on alternative activities that pursue similar learning goals. In this sense, UDL does not replace XAI nor solve the technical challenges of monitoring complex systems. Instead, it creates conditions under which explainability becomes pedagogically actionable. Transparency is no longer merely informational; it becomes embedded within a diversified design that preserves professional judgment and mitigates excessive dependency on automated decision-making.
Importantly, this perspective does not frame UDL as a substitute for algorithmic improvement. The technical development of more robust and interpretable AI systems remains essential. However, educational resilience cannot be guaranteed by technical refinement alone. It also depends on how AI tools are situated within classroom practice. By promoting pluralism, flexibility, and multiple avenues for participation and assessment, UDL offers a design-oriented complement to XAI, reinforcing human oversight not only at the level of decision review but at the structural level of instructional organization.
4.3. The Case of Intelligent Tutoring Systems
A clear illustration of these dynamics can be found in intelligent tutoring systems, commonly referred to as ITS. These systems use algorithmic models to adapt the sequence and difficulty of tasks presented to each student based on their previous performance (Ortega-Morla et al., 2025). In many cases, the system determines which problem a learner encounters next, how long they remain at a given level, and when they are allowed to progress.
When implemented without a broader pedagogical framework, such systems can become the primary structuring force of classroom activity. Although teachers may supervise their use, the main learning trajectory is effectively determined by the algorithm. Even if each recommendation is accompanied by an explanation, the cumulative direction of learning is largely shaped by automated decisions.
However, when the use of an ITS is embedded within a classroom design inspired by diversified instructional principles, its role changes significantly. Algorithmic recommendations become one possible pathway among others rather than the exclusive route. Teachers may combine ITS-generated problems with teacher-designed tasks, collaborative activities, or alternative assessment formats that address the same objectives. Students may demonstrate understanding through multiple modes of expression rather than exclusively through system-assigned exercises.
Under these conditions, explainability gains practical significance. The teacher can interpret the system’s recommendations within a broader pedagogical structure and retain the capacity to redirect, complement, or counterbalance algorithmic guidance when necessary. The ITS does not disappear, but it ceases to function as a single point of pedagogical determination.

4.4. Illustrative Implementation: A Supervised XAI-Based Exercise Generator
To illustrate how the principles discussed above can be operationalized in practice, we briefly present an instructional exercise generator designed under a supervised XAI architecture. The system, referred to here as EduXAI Studio, does not aim to function as an intelligent tutoring system, nor does it model individual learners longitudinally. Instead, it supports teachers in generating exercises while embedding explainability, pedagogical invariants, and structured human oversight within the design process. 
The core premise of the tool is that explainability should not be limited to providing justifications for isolated outputs. Rather, it should be integrated into the architecture of instructional design itself. To this end, the system separates the generation of exercises from the layers that make their pedagogical logic visible and reviewable. Each generated exercise is accompanied by a structured pedagogical summary that explicitly states the learning objective, Bloom level, difficulty, and core conceptual focus. These elements are treated as pedagogical invariants: they must remain stable across any variations introduced. By making these invariants explicit, the system ensures that variability does not undermine curricular coherence.
In addition to this pedagogical layer, the tool provides a distinct explainability layer that includes a rationale for why a specific exercise type was selected, references to content sources, and potential limitations or contextual considerations. This separation between pedagogical summary and technical XAI detail reflects a principle of progressive explainability. Teachers are not confronted with raw model information, but with structured explanations that can be reviewed, interpreted, and, if necessary, questioned or revised. The design thus reinforces the role of the teacher as the final decision-maker.
The system also incorporates conditional variability inspired by Universal Design for Learning. Rather than personalizing content dynamically based on student data, it allows teachers to declare general contextual parameters such as group heterogeneity, expected barriers, or instructional purpose. Based on these inputs, the generator can produce controlled variants of the same exercise while maintaining the predefined pedagogical invariants. In this way, variability is structural and anticipatory rather than reactive and individualized. The algorithm does not determine learning trajectories over time; it offers alternative pathways that remain subject to teacher selection and adaptation.
Additionally, the system produces a transparency report summarizing key aspects of the generated set, including pedagogical alignment, detected risks, potential biases, and an explicit indication that human review is required before classroom use. This reporting layer illustrates how explainability can be connected to governance practices. Transparency is not treated as a passive feature of the model, but as part of a workflow that institutionalizes professional oversight. An example of this reporting layer is shown in Figure 5, where pedagogical alignment, detected risks, UDL variability, and mandatory human review are explicitly presented as part of the workflow.
This example does not claim to resolve the structural limits of monitoring complex AI systems discussed earlier. Rather, it demonstrates that explainability can be embedded at the level of instructional design, not only at the level of algorithmic inference. By combining explicit pedagogical invariants, controlled variability, progressive explanations, and mandatory human review, the system embodies the type of supervised and modular AI architecture that this paper argues should be prioritized in educational contexts.

4.5. UDL as a Practical Criterion for Teachers
From the perspective of everyday classroom practice, considering UDL as a pedagogical safeguard translates into a set of reflective criteria rather than technical procedures. When integrating AI tools, teachers may ask whether the learning objectives addressed by the system can still be achieved through alternative pathways if the tool becomes unavailable. They may also consider whether repeated algorithmic recommendations risk narrowing the range of activities offered to students, and whether complementary tasks are available that allow learners to demonstrate understanding in diverse ways.
Further consideration concerns the distribution of decision-making authority within the classroom. While AI systems can assist in assigning tasks or suggesting activities, teachers retain responsibility for ensuring that students have meaningful opportunities to choose, to engage with content through different modalities, and to progress along varied pedagogical routes. In this sense, UDL operates less as a technical framework and more as a practical lens through which educators can evaluate the extent to which AI tools are integrated without becoming structurally dominant.
These reflections do not require advanced expertise in artificial intelligence. They require professional judgment in instructional design. The central principle remains that AI should function as one component within a diversified learning environment rather than as the exclusive channel through which educational activity is organized.
5. Conclusions
The incorporation of AI into educational settings does not alter a fundamental principle: responsibility for pedagogical decisions ultimately remains with teachers and educational institutions. In this context, explainability is not an optional technical enhancement but a necessary condition for exercising professional judgment without uncritical delegation to algorithmic systems.
This paper argues that while explainable AI is essential, it may not be sufficient when complex systems operate continuously and evolve over time. Even when individual outputs are accompanied by coherent explanations, the cumulative influence of algorithmic decisions can shape learning trajectories in ways that are not immediately visible. For this reason, human oversight must extend beyond reviewing isolated recommendations and include attention to broader patterns of use and structural dependency.
From a practical standpoint, teachers and institutions can adopt several guiding principles. They can critically examine whether AI tools provide explanations that are intelligible and pedagogically relevant. They can monitor how recommendations accumulate over time and reflect on their impact on student confidence, participation, and perceived competence. They can maintain alternative instructional routes that prevent learning from becoming exclusively mediated by a single algorithmic logic. And they can design classroom environments informed by UDL principles, ensuring multiple forms of representation, expression, and engagement that preserve flexibility and professional discretion.
The combination of explainable AI and diversified instructional design does not eliminate uncertainty, nor does it fully resolve the structural limits of monitoring complex systems. However, it establishes conditions under which AI integration becomes more resilient, transparent, and aligned with educational values. Rather than prioritizing increasingly autonomous systems, educational contexts may benefit from emphasizing supervised, modular, and explainable architectures that reinforce human agency. In this way, AI can function not as a substitute for professional judgment, but as a structured support embedded within a plural and pedagogically grounded learning environment.
